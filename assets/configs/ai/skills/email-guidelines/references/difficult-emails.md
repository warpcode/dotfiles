# Difficult Emails — Reference Guide

Use this guide when the email topic involves any of the following:
- Conflict or disagreement with a colleague, manager, or client
- Pushing back on a decision or request
- Complaints (giving or receiving)
- Delivering bad news (missed deadlines, project failure, price increases)
- Sensitive HR situations (performance, absence, redundancy)
- Escalation

---

## General Principles for Difficult Emails

1. **Write the emotional version, then throw it away** — encourage the user to vent first if needed, but never send the venting draft
2. **Describe impact, not blame** — "This caused a two-day delay for our team" not "You didn't do your job"
3. **Separate facts from feelings** — state what happened objectively before expressing concern
4. **Give the other party an easy path forward** — make it easy to respond constructively
5. **Don't bury the issue** — be clear about what the problem is; don't hide it in paragraph three
6. **Read it aloud before sending** — if it sounds harsh out loud, it'll read harsher in writing

---

## Conflict / Disagreement

**Goal:** Express your position clearly without damaging the relationship.

Structure:
1. Acknowledge their perspective or the shared goal
2. State your concern or disagreement specifically
3. Explain the impact or reasoning
4. Invite dialogue or propose a solution

Language to use:
- *"I want to make sure we're aligned on..."*
- *"I have some concerns I'd like to share..."*
- *"My understanding was... — could you help me clarify?"*

Language to avoid:
- *"You're wrong about..."*
- *"As I already said..."*
- *"This is unacceptable"* (unless genuinely warranted)

---

## Pushback / Declining a Request

**Goal:** Say no (or not yet) without burning goodwill.

Structure:
1. Acknowledge the request warmly
2. Explain the constraint (capacity, priority, policy — be honest but brief)
3. Offer an alternative if possible (different timeline, partial help, redirect)
4. Keep the door open

Language:
- *"I really appreciate you thinking of me for this..."*
- *"Unfortunately I'm at capacity right now — would [X timeline] work instead?"*
- *"I'm not best placed for this, but [person] might be..."*

---

## Complaints (Sending)

**Goal:** Get resolution without alienating the recipient.

Structure:
1. State the issue clearly and factually (no emotion)
2. Describe the impact on you/your team/the project
3. State what you need (specific resolution, not just venting)
4. Set a reasonable timeline if needed

Language:
- *"I wanted to flag an issue I've encountered..."*
- *"The impact of this has been [specific effect]..."*
- *"What I'd find helpful is..."*

---

## Delivering Bad News

**Goal:** Be honest, clear, and empathetic — without over-explaining.

Structure:
1. Lead with the news (don't bury it)
2. Briefly explain what happened
3. State what you're doing to address it
4. Offer to discuss

Rules:
- Don't open with apologies before the actual news — it reads as evasive
- Don't over-justify — it looks defensive
- Own the situation: *"We missed the deadline"* not *"Due to unforeseen circumstances the deadline was missed"*

---

## Escalation

**Goal:** Raise an issue to a higher level without looking like you're going around someone.

Best practice:
- Inform the person being escalated over first, if possible ("I've copied [manager] as they'll want visibility on this")
- Stick to facts and impact — no editorial about whose fault it is
- Be clear about what you're asking the senior person to do (decision, awareness, intervention)
- Keep it brief — executives scan; they don't read

---

## Tone Ladder (Escalating Firmness)

Use this to calibrate how firm the email should be:

| Level | Tone | Example phrase |
|---|---|---|
| 1 – Gentle | Curious, light | *"Just wanted to check in on this..."* |
| 2 – Polite chase | Friendly, direct | *"Could you let me know the status by [date]?"* |
| 3 – Firm | Clear, professional | *"I need this by [date] to keep the project on track."* |
| 4 – Formal complaint | Serious, documented | *"I'm formally raising this as a concern..."* |
| 5 – Escalation | Executive summary, factual | *"I'm bringing this to your attention as it is affecting [outcome]."* |

Match the level to the history and stakes. Never jump from Level 1 to Level 4 — it reads as disproportionate.
